Design Theme · Active Living

Turning club runs into the easiest healthy habit in your week.

RunLink is a club-scoped mobile web app: runners prove attendance with a location-based map check-in (Haversine · fixed 50 m), follow the organizer’s route overlaid on the full-screen Running map (Leaflet, OpenStreetMap tiles, GeoJSON polyline), then leave lightweight club-only reflections (emoji + note) on the activity — never a global social feed. Organizers publish activities, upload GPX/route data, watch a live registration/check-in dashboard, and rely on Supabase (Postgres + Row Level Security) for real data behind the static front end.

01 · Motivation & Research

Why Active Living, and the gap we found in it.

Before shipping a single pixel, we spent two weeks on interviews, academic reading, and competitor teardowns. We picked the Active Living theme because the barrier to consistent adult exercise is almost never willpower — it's logistics, loneliness, and loops that break on a rainy Tuesday.

The Why — Our Motivation

We chose Active Living because every team member has at some point signed up for a gym, a 10k, or a "new year I'll actually run" pact — and quietly dropped it within six weeks. The WHO reports that 27.5% of adults fall short of recommended activity levels, and the strongest mitigating factor in the literature is not another fitness tracker but a social commitment device: a small group that notices when you don't show up. Running clubs already exist in every city, yet the apps we tried treat them as either private chat rooms (Telegram/WeChat groups) or anonymous global leaderboards (Strava segments). Neither makes it easy to walk out the door on Saturday at 6:45 AM and know exactly where 12 other humans will be waiting. Our product bet: if we can compress the entire "register → remember → show up → record → reflect" loop into a mobile web app that never asks more than one tap at a time, casual runners will stay with a club, and clubs will retain members long enough for habit formation to actually happen. — 211 words, team position statement

Research Plan

  • 7 user interviews with active runners (3) and lapsed runners (4), 30–45 min each.
  • 2 field visits to a Saturday park-run meetup + a weekday track session.
  • 5 peer-reviewed papers covering behavior change, social exercise, and mobile HCI.
  • 4 commercial teardowns (Strava, Nike Run Club, Keep, JoinClubs).
  • 3 usability rounds on Figma low-fi, early RunLink build, and the deployed Supabase-backed app.

The Gap — 4 Academic Papers

Each paper is summarized as 3 things it gets right and 3 gaps that shaped RunLink's design decisions. Full citations appear in §References.

P1
Consolvo et al. — "Design Requirements for Technologies that Encourage Physical Activity" (CHI '06)
Foundational work on ambient encouragement in fitness UIs.
What it does well
  • Argues movement feedback should be non-judgmental and ambient — we adopted this for reflections/emoji.
  • Identifies "social influence" as a stronger lever than raw data dashboards.
  • Shows that goals work best when group-set, not app-set.
What it misses
  • 2006 mobile context — no GPS / no geofencing for on-site check-in.
  • No discussion of what happens when users miss a session (our "Missed" lifecycle).
  • Doesn't consider privacy of real-time location in a social group.
P2
Lindqvist, Cranshaw et al. — "I'm the Mayor of My House: Examining Why People Use Foursquare" (CHI '11)
Canonical study of location check-ins as a social game.
What it does well
  • Shows check-ins succeed when there is a person to check in to, not a system.
  • Gamified presence but stopped short of competitive leaderboards — matches our monthly-km cap.
  • Identifies "ambient sociality" — knowing a friend is nearby without explicit chat.
What it misses
  • No notion of a time window — Foursquare check-in is anytime, ours is tied to start/end.
  • Gaming the check-in ("I was kind of nearby") is unchecked — we added 50 m geofence + GPS confidence fallback.
  • No resilience story for GPS failure (urban canyon, permission denied).
P3
Munson & Consolvo — "Exploring Goal-Setting, Rewards, Self-Monitoring, and Sharing to Motivate Physical Activity" (PervasiveHealth '12)
A/B tested sharing patterns inside a fitness tracker.
What it does well
  • Sharing progress with a small, known group beats broadcast sharing by 2× adherence.
  • Short reflective notes beat photos for emotional stickiness — inspired our 200-char reflections.
  • Rewards should be social, not monetary.
What it misses
  • The study group was pre-existing friends; our users' "club" is looser and needs scaffolding.
  • No handling of lapsed users — we added the "Checked in · no run recorded" state.
  • No discussion of organizer/moderator load.
P4
Stragier, Vanden Abeele et al. — "Recreational Athletes' Running Motivations as Predictors of Sharing Behavior on Facebook" (Comput. Hum. Behav., 2016)
Survey of 466 runners on why they share (or don't) their runs.
What it does well
  • Shows intrinsic-motivated runners resent public kudos economies.
  • Quantifies "accountability sharing" as a distinct motivation from "performance bragging".
  • Suggests club-scoped, private sharing as a viable middle ground — our Community tab is exactly this.
What it misses
  • Facebook-era — no mobile check-in context, no activity lifecycle.
  • Only studies why people post, not why they read others' posts (which drives club retention).
  • No data on how free runs vs. organized runs differ in sharing propensity (we split these).

The Gap — 4 Commercial Competitors

C1
Strava
The incumbent. Route library, segments, global kudos economy.
What it does well
  • Best-in-class GPS recording, splits, and heart-rate rendering.
  • Segments create low-commitment rivalry.
  • Flyby/Group runs lets you see who overlapped with you.
What it misses
  • No concept of a scheduled club activity with a start time + meetup pin.
  • No on-site check-in — you can fake an entire run.
  • Community is global; small clubs drown under elite athletes' feeds.
C2
Nike Run Club
Guided runs, coached plans, brand-led community events.
What it does well
  • Guided audio coaching is genuinely great for beginners.
  • Clean training plans with sensible defaults.
  • Beautiful achievement system with modest rewards.
What it misses
  • No user-created clubs — only Nike-organized events.
  • No check-in; no roster of who's running today.
  • Zero organizer tools — one direction only (brand → user).
C3
Keep (China)
Hybrid fitness super-app with group activities and live classes.
What it does well
  • Strong "local community" surface with nearby clubs and meetups.
  • In-app check-in for offline events (QR-based).
  • Leaderboards scoped to the club, not the whole app.
What it misses
  • QR check-in requires another person and a poster — breaks on wet mornings.
  • Commerce-heavy: feed is cluttered with paid classes and merch.
  • Private-first: difficult to join a club without WeChat friends already inside.
C4
parkrun (web + paper)
Worldwide free 5k events; barcode scan at finish.
What it does well
  • Rituals of physical check-in + results email after — powerful emotional loop.
  • 100% free, volunteer-run, gloriously unhurried.
  • Results pages are evergreen — great for long-term motivation.
What it misses
  • Paper barcode is a real friction — left at home = no result.
  • Only 5k Saturdays — no weekday intervals, no trail, no custom distances.
  • No in-app social layer — everything is email + Facebook.

Stakeholders & Personas

RunLink has a deliberately sharp two-sided model: the beginner runner looking for a low-stakes way into a club (primary, ~70% of the market), and the club organizer keeping weekends running (secondary, the ~10–15% power-user tail). A third — the parent club owner — shows up in governance flows (transfer ownership, co-organizer roles, club deletion) in RunLink’s Community tab, but isn't a daily-driver persona.

Three core requirements the system must meet

R1 — One-tap Map check-in

A registered runner must be able to mark themselves present with a single tap, taken from a card on Discover → My registrations. No form, no scanner, no second screen. The system silently auto-acquires GPS, validates the time window, and enforces a fixed 50 m geofence.

RunLink: geofence.js · activity detail check-in

R2 — Activity route on the running map

After check-in, the organizer’s uploaded GPX/KML is stored as route_geojson and rendered with Leaflet on the Running tab’s full-bleed map — entry is the top-right route square, which opens a picker for checked-in activities. Free-run (no linked activity) stays available. While a session is recording, the linked route stays fixed so distance and course don’t drift apart.

RunLink: map.js · views.js Running tab

R3 — Club-scoped reflection

Checked-in runners react with a preset emoji and an optional short note on the activity detail page; others see a chronological reflection feed for that activity only. Nothing is published to a global or cross-club feed — scope stays inside the club’s activity. (Posting is gated until the runner has checked in from Discover → My registrations.)

RunLink: reflections table · RLS · views.js

Design Alternatives — three systems compared

We compared three end-to-end interaction systems for "how a runner proves they showed up". The chosen option (B) survived usability testing; A and C were explicitly killed with recorded rationale.

Option A · Rejected

Organizer-held QR turnstile

The organizer prints / displays a rotating QR at the meetup; runners scan to check in. Heavily inspired by parkrun barcodes and Keep events.

+Visible human moment — a volunteer at the gate.
+Cannot be faked without physical presence.
–Breaks in rain; needs charged phone + poster.
–Dead on day 1 without a volunteer present.
–Two-app flow (scanner → return) violates R1.
✗ Killed — violates "one-tap" and requires volunteer logistics small clubs don't have.
Option B · Chosen

Silent GPS-window check-in (50 m + time window)

A single tap on the Discover "My registrations" card triggers auto-GPS + time-window validation against the organizer's meetup pin. No UI-level knobs. GPS-denied cases fall back to a "Confirm you're at the meetup" dialog.

+Single tap; zero state to manage on the runner's side.
+Fixed 50 m radius — fair across all activities, no organizer guesswork.
+Fallback + audit trail (gps_verified / manual_confirmed / organizer_manual).
–Requires location permission the first time.
✓ Chosen — cleared usability with all 3 testers; visibly reduced organizer load in interviews.
Option C · Rejected

Proof-by-recording (no explicit check-in)

Runner simply starts a run inside the app during the activity window near the meetup pin; the system infers attendance from the recorded track. No check-in button at all.

+Minimalism — one less step in the flow.
+Great telemetry for the organizer's dashboard.
–Runners who chat before starting get "no show" — harsh and wrong.
–Breaks for runners who forget to press Start.
–No moment of "I'm here" — kills the social ritual that motivates showing up.
✗ Killed — confirmed in usability round 1: testers wanted an explicit act of arrival.

04 · Technical Implementation

A boringly reliable mobile web app with an auditable data path.

RunLink is a hash-routed single-page app (HTML/CSS + vanilla ES modules, no bundler) talking to Supabase for auth, Postgres, Row Level Security, and Realtime. Static files can sit on GitHub Pages, Vercel, or Netlify; only the Supabase URL and anon key ship to the client.

05.3 · Final Reflection

Social & ethical implications

RunLink is built around a presence signal — I was here at this time. That is a powerful piece of personal data, especially for women, queer users, and people in locations where gathering is politicized. We deliberately chose to keep the check-in surface club-scoped and never to build a global "who's running now" feed. A club leader has no right to a member's live coordinates. We also capped leaderboards at one month because we saw, even in our own testing, how quickly a lifetime ranking can make a newcomer feel unwelcome.

The honest limit of our design: we assumed a well-functioning club exists. RunLink doesn't solve the cold-start problem — if the nearest club is 40 km away, we can't conjure one. That is acceptable for our scope, but future work should consider lightweight "pop-up" clubs for underserved areas before this becomes yet another tool that benefits only runners in dense, wealthy cities.

What we'd do next

  • Offline-first recording: a service worker to actually survive subway tunnels.
  • Accessibility audit: dynamic type, VoiceOver labels on the map check-in surface, larger tap targets on group chips.
  • Organizer analytics: honest "who is drifting away" nudge without surveillance creep.
  • i18n: Simplified Chinese + English day one; date/time formatting is already tz-aware.
  • Trust controls: finer-grained visibility defaults for club roster and leaderboards.
06 · Technical Reflection · AI Use

How we used AI — and how we kept it honest.

Per the course's mandatory disclosure, we used AI coding assistance (Vibe Coding, specifically Cursor with Claude) during the hi-fi build. Below are the exact prompt patterns we used, how we validated the output, and the ethical questions we debated.

Where AI helped (and where it didn't)

  • Helped: generating repetitive CSS scaffolds, RunLink README and SQL-adjacent prose, SVG architecture diagrams, sketch placeholders.
  • Helped: catching edge cases in the check-in state matrix we missed.
  • Didn't replace: user interviews, field visits, the Crazy-Eights session, usability scoring.
  • Didn't replace: deciding what to kill (Option A, Option C) — that needed human testers.

Validation workflow

  • Every AI-generated module was manually diffed against the behaviour described in our spec and in RunLink/README.md.
  • Usability sessions were run on deployed builds, not AI-generated mockups, so the signal came from real user behavior.
  • All copy that went into a user-facing surface was read aloud by a teammate — AI tends to over-explain; we trimmed aggressively.
  • AI suggestions that affected logic (geofence, time window) were paired with a written unit trace before we accepted them.
  • Nothing was pushed to main without at least one human approver.

Ethical considerations around AI-generated code

Accessibility

AI-generated HTML defaults to div-soup. We manually replaced generated <div>s with semantic <nav>, <main>, <section>, and <article>, added aria-labels on icon-only buttons, honored prefers-reduced-motion, and ensured focus-visible states. AI also tends to use emojis as icons — we replaced them with inline SVGs for screen-reader hygiene.

Bias & representation

Default AI personas skew male, young, and urban. We explicitly re-generated our personas against a checklist (gender, age, device, income bracket, running history) and cross-checked with the seven people we actually interviewed. We also audited AI-generated copy for ableist language around pace and distance — e.g., changing "run faster" defaults to pace-neutral framings.

Privacy & attribution

No real interview transcripts or user data were shared with external LLM services. We used AI for template copy, then hand-stitched in anonymized quotes. All AI-generated code blocks pushed to our repository carry a comment tag // AI-assisted · reviewed by <member> and are listed in the References section of this portfolio.
