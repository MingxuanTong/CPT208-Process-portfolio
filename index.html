Design Theme · Active Living

Turning club runs into the easiest healthy habit in your week.

RunLink is a club-scoped mobile web app: runners prove attendance with a location-based map check-in (Haversine · fixed 50 m), follow the organizer’s route overlaid on the full-screen Running map (Leaflet, OpenStreetMap tiles, GeoJSON polyline), then leave lightweight club-only reflections (emoji + note) on the activity — never a global social feed. Organizers publish activities, upload GPX/route data, watch a live registration/check-in dashboard, and rely on Supabase (Postgres + Row Level Security) for real data behind the static front end.

01 · Motivation & Research

Why Active Living, and the gap we found in it.

Before shipping a single pixel, we spent two weeks on interviews, academic reading, and competitor teardowns. We picked the Active Living theme because the barrier to consistent adult exercise is almost never willpower — it's logistics, loneliness, and loops that break on a rainy Tuesday.

The Why — Our Motivation

We chose Active Living because our team has repeatedly seen the same pattern: people start running with good intentions, then stop when the routine becomes hard to sustain. The WHO reports that 27.5% of adults do not meet recommended activity levels. Our interviews and field visits suggest the core issue is not motivation alone, but broken social routines: people forget sessions, feel awkward joining groups, or lose confidence when they cannot find the meetup flow quickly. Existing apps either emphasize global performance feeds or private chat groups, but rarely support a reliable local club loop from registration to attendance to reflection. RunLink is our response: a club-scoped mobile web app that reduces friction across one weekly loop — register → check in → run → reflect — so more casual runners can return consistently and clubs can retain members long enough for healthy habits to form.

Research Plan

  • 7 user interviews with active runners (3) and lapsed runners (4), 30–45 min each.
  • 2 field visits to a Saturday park-run meetup + a weekday track session.
  • 5 peer-reviewed papers covering behavior change, social exercise, and mobile HCI.
  • 4 commercial teardowns (Strava, Nike Run Club, Keep, JoinClubs).
  • 3 usability rounds on Figma low-fi, early RunLink build, and the deployed Supabase-backed app.

The Gap — Academic Papers

4 papers reviewed to analyse check-in, social motivation, attendance reliability, and club-scoped engagement patterns.

01
Paper 01 — Consolvo et al. (CHI '06)
Relevance: Foundational work on ambient encouragement in fitness UIs.
Strengths
  • Argues movement feedback should be non-judgmental and ambient — we adopted this for reflections/emoji.
  • Identifies "social influence" as a stronger lever than raw data dashboards.
  • Shows that goals work best when group-set, not app-set.
Limitations
  • 2006 mobile context — no GPS / no geofencing for on-site check-in.
  • No discussion of what happens when users miss a session (our "Missed" lifecycle).
  • Doesn't consider privacy of real-time location in a social group.
02
Paper 02 — Lindqvist et al. (CHI '11)
Relevance: Canonical study of location check-ins as a social game.
Strengths
  • Shows check-ins succeed when there is a person to check in to, not a system.
  • Gamified presence but stopped short of competitive leaderboards — matches our monthly-km cap.
  • Identifies "ambient sociality" — knowing a friend is nearby without explicit chat.
Limitations
  • No notion of a time window — Foursquare check-in is anytime, ours is tied to start/end.
  • Gaming the check-in ("I was kind of nearby") is unchecked — we added 50 m geofence + GPS confidence fallback.
  • No resilience story for GPS failure (urban canyon, permission denied).
03
Paper 03 — A Game-based AR Navigation System for Makerspace Education
Chen, Hwang & Lin (2023) · Relevance: Shows how AR and gamification can support active exploration.
Strengths
  • Combines AR with gamified missions and rewards.
  • Provides a clear and structured interaction flow.
  • Supports active learning through problem-solving tasks.
Limitations
  • May prioritise engagement over learning depth.
  • Context is limited to a library makerspace.
  • Interaction design may be too complex for general mobile users.
04
Paper 04 — Stragier et al. (2016)
Relevance: Survey of 466 runners on why they share (or don't) their runs.
Strengths
  • Shows intrinsic-motivated runners resent public kudos economies.
  • Quantifies "accountability sharing" as a distinct motivation from "performance bragging".
  • Suggests club-scoped, private sharing as a viable middle ground — our Community tab is exactly this.
Limitations
  • Facebook-era — no mobile check-in context, no activity lifecycle.
  • Only studies why people post, not why they read others' posts (which drives club retention).
  • No data on how free runs vs. organized runs differ in sharing propensity (we split these).

Commercial Products

4 products reviewed to analyse navigation, social loops, exploration, and organizer/runner interaction patterns.

01
Product 01 — Strava
Relevance: Route library + social running baseline for comparison.
Strengths
  • Best-in-class GPS recording, splits, and heart-rate rendering.
  • Segments create low-commitment rivalry.
  • Flyby/Group runs lets you see who overlapped with you.
Limitations
  • No concept of a scheduled club activity with a start time + meetup pin.
  • No on-site check-in — you can fake an entire run.
  • Community is global; small clubs drown under elite athletes' feeds.
02
Product 02 — Nike Run Club
Relevance: Strong beginner coaching and habit framing in a mainstream running app.
Strengths
  • Guided audio coaching is genuinely great for beginners.
  • Clean training plans with sensible defaults.
  • Beautiful achievement system with modest rewards.
Limitations
  • No user-created clubs — only Nike-organized events.
  • No check-in; no roster of who's running today.
  • Zero organizer tools — one direction only (brand → user).
03
Product 03 — Keep
Relevance: Local community + event mechanics in a China-context product.
Strengths
  • Strong "local community" surface with nearby clubs and meetups.
  • In-app check-in for offline events (QR-based).
  • Leaderboards scoped to the club, not the whole app.
Limitations
  • QR check-in requires another person and a poster — breaks on wet mornings.
  • Commerce-heavy: feed is cluttered with paid classes and merch.
  • Private-first: difficult to join a club without WeChat friends already inside.
04
Product 04 — parkrun
Relevance: Large-scale offline run ritual and attendance verification model.
Strengths
  • Rituals of physical check-in + results email after — powerful emotional loop.
  • 100% free, volunteer-run, gloriously unhurried.
  • Results pages are evergreen — great for long-term motivation.
Limitations
  • Paper barcode is a real friction — left at home = no result.
  • Only 5k Saturdays — no weekday intervals, no trail, no custom distances.
  • No in-app social layer — everything is email + Facebook.

Gap Summary

What existing work does well and what it still misses.

3 Things They Did Well

  • They show strong potential for combining route guidance with place-aware context.
  • They support self-paced participation and active exploration behavior.
  • They show social and playful interactions can improve engagement.

3 Things They Missed

  • They rarely model runner and organizer roles together in one coherent system flow.
  • They under-specify attendance reliability under GPS failure and strict time-window constraints.
  • They offer limited club-scoped reflection, feedback, and discussion tied to each activity.

Stakeholders & Personas

RunLink has a deliberately sharp two-sided model: the beginner runner looking for a low-stakes way into a club (primary, ~70% of the market), and the club organizer keeping weekends running (secondary, the ~10–15% power-user tail). A third — the parent club owner — shows up in governance flows (transfer ownership, co-organizer roles, club deletion) in RunLink’s Community tab, but isn't a daily-driver persona.

02 · User Requirements

From lived friction to three non-negotiable features.

We mapped Tom's Saturday stage-by-stage — and on the other side of the platform, Hanqin's weekly organizer workflow — then highlighted the points each one silently gives up, and distilled that into a short list of "if this isn't true, we haven't shipped."

Three core requirements the system must meet

R1 — One-tap Map check-in

A registered runner must be able to mark themselves present with a single tap, taken from a card on Discover → My registrations. No form, no scanner, no second screen. The system silently auto-acquires GPS, validates the time window, and enforces a fixed 50 m geofence.

RunLink: geofence.js · activity detail check-in

R2 — Activity route on the running map

After check-in, the organizer’s uploaded GPX/KML is stored as route_geojson and rendered with Leaflet on the Running tab’s full-bleed map — entry is the top-right route square, which opens a picker for checked-in activities. Free-run (no linked activity) stays available. While a session is recording, the linked route stays fixed so distance and course don’t drift apart.

RunLink: map.js · views.js Running tab

R3 — Club-scoped reflection

Checked-in runners react with a preset emoji and an optional short note on the activity detail page; others see a chronological reflection feed for that activity only. Nothing is published to a global or cross-club feed — scope stays inside the club’s activity. (Posting is gated until the runner has checked in from Discover → My registrations.)

RunLink: reflections table · RLS · views.js

Spotlight — Professional Club Operations

Beyond the core runner loop (check-in → route on map → reflect), RunLink ships organizer tooling backed by real Postgres data and RLS — not hand-seeded JSON. The six capabilities below mirror what the shipped app does today.

Secondary persona: Hanqin Wan · Owner / Co-organizer

Publish activity in ≈ 2 min

Schedule with start / end + check-in window, upload a GPX/KML route, set per-group pace options with optional per-group capacity, and an optional total capacity. Helper copy enforces the 50 m geofence rule — one less knob to misconfigure.

views.js publish · api.js · route → GeoJSON

Live data dashboard

Per-activity counts: registrations (n / capacity_total when capped), check-ins, Late, and Cancelled. Per-group breakdown when per-group caps are set. Every check-in row is audit-tagged gps_verified, manual_confirmed, or organizer_manual.

views.js dashboard · organizer-only

Manual mark check-in

For runners whose GPS failed inside the window, organizers can tap Mark in on a not-checked-in row. Runner-side “Confirm you’re at the meetup” when accuracy is poor is logged for organizer review.

api.manualCheckIn · check-in methods

Role-based governance

Exactly one Owner per club. Any number of Co-organizers (publish + dashboard + manual check-in + album). Members browse and join. Ownership can be transferred; destructive actions stay owner-gated.

club_memberships · roles in api.js

Club-scoped monthly leaderboard

Current-month distance ranking for the active club only — gold / silver / bronze-style ranks and tier badges, not a global Strava-style board.

v_monthly_mileage · Community tab

Realtime club awareness

Supabase Realtime subscription: when someone registers for an activity you can see, a toast fires (“Someone just joined…”). Reinforces club presence without exposing live coordinates or a public feed.

postgres_changes · app.js

Crazy Eights — 8 minutes, 8 UI directions

Design Alternatives — three system models compared

Three system-level models — each with its own check-in mechanism, power balance, and community loop. Option B alone satisfies all persona pain points.

Option A · Rejected

Organizer-Centralised Model

Organizer as gatekeeper: QR scan at the meetup, manual attendance verification, exclusive control of publishing and community content. Runners are passive consumers.

+Physical QR scan — strong attendance integrity.
+Visible "welcome moment" at the gate.
–Single point of failure: no volunteer → no check-in.
–Two-device flow violates "one-tap" requirement.
–Amplifies organiser burden (O1: 45+ min/week on logistics).
–Passive runners disengage — "Nobody noticed I stopped".
✗ Killed — small clubs cannot sustain volunteer-gate logistics; violates "reduce organiser load" requirement.
Option B · Chosen

Peer-Community Model

GPS self-check-in (one tap, 50 m + time window). Shared community tools: reflections, reactions, co-organizer role. Organiser shifts from gatekeeper to enabler.

+One-tap check-in — no volunteer needed.
+Audit trail (gps_verified / manual_confirmed / organiser_manual).
+Co-organizer distributes workload (addresses O1).
+Reflections & reactions give runners agency (addresses P1).
–Requires location permission on first use.
–Self-check-in theoretically spoofable — mitigated by 50 m + time-window constraints.
✓ Chosen — reduces organiser burden (O1), gives runners agency (P1), keeps attendance reliable (P2). Cleared usability with all 3 testers.
Option C · Rejected

Runner-First Individual Model

Personal tracking is core; clubs are a lightweight overlay. Attendance inferred from GPS tracks near the meetup — no explicit check-in. Minimal community features.

+No check-in step — "just run" philosophy.
+Rich personal telemetry for data-driven runners.
+Simpler system — fewer edge cases.
–No "I'm here" moment — testers wanted an arrival act.
–Socialising before Start = "no show" — punitive for club culture.
–Organiser has zero operational visibility.
–Misaligned with brief — this is a club companion, not a solo tracker.
✗ Killed — contradicts project brief; inferred attendance punishes social behaviour; no organiser insight.

04 · Technical Implementation

A boringly reliable mobile web app with an auditable data path.

RunLink is a hash-routed single-page app (HTML/CSS + vanilla ES modules, no bundler) talking to Supabase for auth, Postgres, Row Level Security, and Realtime. Static files can sit on GitHub Pages, Vercel, or Netlify; only the Supabase URL and anon key ship to the client.

Tech stack at a glance

  • Frontend: SPA shell (index.html), styles/app.css, ES modules in scripts/ — no bundler.
  • Routing: app.js hash router + views.js render functions.
  • Backend: Supabase Auth + Postgres; api.js typed data access; every table protected by RLS.
  • Maps: Leaflet + OSM (or CARTO dark) tiles; map.js addRouteGeoJson for club routes on the Running tab.
  • Check-in: geofence.js Haversine · default 50 m; GPS fallback + organizer manual mark.
  • Realtime: Supabase postgres_changes for registration toasts; optional CI deploy to GitHub Pages / Vercel.

05.2 · Iterative Refinement

Before → After · usability-driven iterations

These snapshots come from our research and usability rounds as we tightened RunLink — the same product story you can run in the repo today (hash router, Supabase, Leaflet route overlay).

Iteration 1 · Fairness

Check-in radius: configurable → fixed 50 m

Before
Publish activity
  • Radius slider 25 m — 500 m
  • Default 100 m
  • Organizers accidentally set 500 m "to be nice"
  • Runners exploited wide radius
→
After
Publish activity
  • No radius field at all
  • Helper text: "Fixed 50 m, system-wide"
  • Schema constant CHECK_IN_RADIUS_M = 50
  • Fair across every activity
Why: Tester 2 and an organizer tester both raised trust concerns. Removing the knob made the system simpler and fairer.
Iteration 2 · Single check-in surface

Activity detail: informational only — no check-in

Before
Activity detail · Registered
  • Big "Map check-in" CTA
  • Check-in countdown card
  • "Go run" primary when checked in
  • Duplicated with Discover
→
After
Activity detail · Registered
  • "Registered" badge only
  • Schedule tiles, no countdown
  • Sticky CTA: "View in My registrations"
  • Single source of truth for check-in
Why: Tester 1 tried to check in on detail and was confused when nothing happened after returning to Discover. Consolidating removed the ambiguity.
Iteration 3 · Running map · route UI

Activity route entry: full bar → top-right square only

Before
Running tab
  • Full-width "Activity route" bar below the top bar
  • Visually split the map in two panels
  • Route could be swapped mid-run
  • Testers tapped the bar expecting a check-in
→
After
Running tab
  • One continuous full-screen map
  • Single 44 px square in the top-right as the only entry
  • Route locked during Recording (stats integrity)
  • "None" = free run, explicit and honest
Why: Tester 1 and Tester 2 both tried to use the route bar as a second check-in affordance — reinforcing that check-in belongs on Discover. Collapsing the entry also gave the map back its full canvas.
Iteration 4 · My activities unity

Two histories merged → one "My activities" page

Before
History surfaces
  • Discover → "See all" = list A
  • Profile → "My registrations" = list B
  • Different filters, different sort
  • Testers couldn't predict each
→
After
History surfaces
  • Both entries → same page
  • Exactly 2 tabs: Upcoming, Past
  • No "All", no club filter, no "Today · Now"
  • Deep-link parity preserved

05.3 · Final Reflection

Social & ethical implications

RunLink is built around a presence signal — I was here at this time. That is a powerful piece of personal data, especially for women, queer users, and people in locations where gathering is politicized. We deliberately chose to keep the check-in surface club-scoped and never to build a global "who's running now" feed. A club leader has no right to a member's live coordinates. We also capped leaderboards at one month because we saw, even in our own testing, how quickly a lifetime ranking can make a newcomer feel unwelcome.

The honest limit of our design: we assumed a well-functioning club exists. RunLink doesn't solve the cold-start problem — if the nearest club is 40 km away, we can't conjure one. That is acceptable for our scope, but future work should consider lightweight "pop-up" clubs for underserved areas before this becomes yet another tool that benefits only runners in dense, wealthy cities.

What we'd do next

  • Offline-first recording: a service worker to actually survive subway tunnels.
  • Accessibility audit: dynamic type, VoiceOver labels on the map check-in surface, larger tap targets on group chips.
  • Organizer analytics: honest "who is drifting away" nudge without surveillance creep.
  • i18n: Simplified Chinese + English day one; date/time formatting is already tz-aware.
  • Trust controls: finer-grained visibility defaults for club roster and leaderboards.
06 · Technical Reflection · AI Use

How we used AI — and how we kept it honest.

Per the course's mandatory disclosure, we used AI coding assistance (Vibe Coding, specifically Cursor with Claude) during the hi-fi build. Below are the exact prompt patterns we used, how we validated the output, and the ethical questions we debated.

Where AI helped (and where it didn't)

  • Helped: generating repetitive CSS scaffolds, RunLink README and SQL-adjacent prose, SVG architecture diagrams, sketch placeholders.
  • Helped: catching edge cases in the check-in state matrix we missed.
  • Didn't replace: user interviews, field visits, the Crazy-Eights session, usability scoring.
  • Didn't replace: deciding what to kill (Option A, Option C) — that needed human testers.

Validation workflow

  • Every AI-generated module was manually diffed against the behaviour described in our spec and in RunLink/README.md.
  • Usability sessions were run on deployed builds, not AI-generated mockups, so the signal came from real user behavior.
  • All copy that went into a user-facing surface was read aloud by a teammate — AI tends to over-explain; we trimmed aggressively.
  • AI suggestions that affected logic (geofence, time window) were paired with a written unit trace before we accepted them.
  • Nothing was pushed to main without at least one human approver.

Example prompts used for core logic

Prompt 1 — Check-in state matrix

// Generating the 6-state button matrix for the registration card > You are helping me design a registration card button matrix. Inputs: checked_in (bool), now, check_in_start, check_in_end, start_time. Output 6 mutually exclusive states with: - button label - disabled / enabled - style (primary-green, primary-amber, ghost, etc.) - the exact condition as a boolean expression State names should align with RunLink: activity lifecycle (open / registered / checked_in / past / cancelled) plus the check-in window relative to now.

Prompt 2 — Haversine geofence

// Validating "within 50 m of meetup" for map check-in > Write a small vanilla JS function isWithinCheckinRadius(userLat, userLng, meetupLat, meetupLng) that returns true iff the great-circle distance is <= 50 meters. Use Haversine. Include a JSDoc block, cite the formula, and add a 3-case unit trace in a comment block. Do not use any map library.

Prompt 3 — Portfolio prose

// Generating research prose from raw interview notes > I will paste 7 interview notes below. Produce a 200-word "motivation statement" that (a) names the theme Active Living, (b) quotes one actual user pain, (c) positions our product against apps that treat clubs as chat rooms or anonymous leaderboards, (d) ends with a measurable bet. Do not invent statistics; if you need a number, leave [BRACKET] for me to fill.

Prompt 4 — GPS fallback copy

// Writing the "confirm you're at the meetup" dialog copy > Write a short, warm, non-accusatory dialog for a case where the app cannot verify location but the runner insists they are at the meetup. It must: (1) acknowledge the failure is on us, (2) name the meetup explicitly, (3) ask only one self-confirm action, (4) mention that the organizer will see a "self-confirmed" chip. Max 40 words.

Ethical considerations around AI-generated code

Accessibility

AI-generated HTML defaults to div-soup. We manually replaced generated <div>s with semantic <nav>, <main>, <section>, and <article>, added aria-labels on icon-only buttons, honored prefers-reduced-motion, and ensured focus-visible states. AI also tends to use emojis as icons — we replaced them with inline SVGs for screen-reader hygiene.

Bias & representation

Default AI personas skew male, young, and urban. We explicitly re-generated our personas against a checklist (gender, age, device, income bracket, running history) and cross-checked with the seven people we actually interviewed. We also audited AI-generated copy for ableist language around pace and distance — e.g., changing "run faster" defaults to pace-neutral framings.

Privacy & attribution

No real interview transcripts or user data were shared with external LLM services. We used AI for template copy, then hand-stitched in anonymized quotes. All AI-generated code blocks pushed to our repository carry a comment tag // AI-assisted · reviewed by <member> and are listed in the References section of this portfolio.
